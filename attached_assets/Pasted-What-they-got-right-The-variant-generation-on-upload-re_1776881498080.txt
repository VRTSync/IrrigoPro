What they got right
The variant-generation-on-upload recommendation is the correct top priority. That's textbook image handling and the math they're citing (400MB to render 200px tiles) is not an exaggeration — that's genuinely what's happening. Fixing HEIC-once-not-every-request is also obviously correct; re-running sharp on every request is wasteful and adds real latency on cold requests.
Batch-signing URLs is a legit win too. One API roundtrip per photo in a grid is death by a thousand cuts.
Things I'd push on before you accept the task as scoped
Field conditions matter a lot here. This is a field capture app — techs are probably on spotty LTE in rural areas, sometimes offline. That changes the priority order:

Upload-side compression is arguably more important than variant generation for your use case. If a tech in a field with one bar is uploading a 4MB original, that upload is slow and flaky. Client-side resize-before-upload (cap at 2048px, re-encode as JPEG ~85 quality) means the original that hits GCS is already ~500KB. Replit lumped this into #4 but treated it as a storage-cost issue; for you it's a reliability issue.
Ask whether they're preserving the true original anywhere. For irrigation work, photos may be evidence (damage claims, pre/post install documentation, warranty disputes). If you compress at upload and lose EXIF, that could bite you later. You might want: compressed-and-stripped for display/PDFs, plus an untouched original kept in cold storage (GCS Nearline/Coldline is cheap).

On HEIC conversion: agree it should be done once, but also consider just converting client-side before upload. Browsers and iOS Safari can do this now and it avoids server CPU entirely. Worth asking.
On the CDN point (#5): they're underselling this. For a field app where the same photos get viewed repeatedly (tech uploads, office reviews, customer sees them in an invoice, warranty claim references them a year later), a CDN isn't a nice-to-have — it's the thing that makes the app feel fast for anyone not on fiber. I wouldn't leave it as "separate follow-up" forever; I'd do it right after the variant work.
On PDF generation (#6): good catch on their part. One extra thing — if invoices are ever emailed, bloated PDFs get rejected by mail servers (25MB Gmail limit). This might be a silent bug in production already.
Questions worth asking Replit before they start

What variants specifically — what dimensions, what quality, what format? (Suggest: thumb 400px, medium 1200px, original preserved. JPEG quality 80-85. Consider WebP with JPEG fallback.)
Will they strip EXIF on the display variants but keep GPS/timestamp on the preserved original? (GPS on field photos is often useful for audit trails.)
How are they handling the migration of existing photos? Backfill job, or lazy-convert on next access?
What's the plan if variant generation fails partway (e.g., upload succeeds but thumb generation crashes)? Retry queue, or serve the original as fallback?
Are uploads resumable? For field techs on bad connections this matters more than you'd think.

My take on whether to accept the task
Yes, but expand the scope slightly to include client-side pre-upload compression, and get clarity on the original-preservation question before they write code. Those two decisions are hard to reverse later. The CDN I'd actually bump up in priority if any of your users are viewing photos on mobile data.
One more thing worth flagging: you mentioned you have more context to share. If you can tell me who the users are (just field techs, or also office/admin/customers?), rough photo volume per work order, and whether these photos ever need to be produced for legal/warranty purposes, I can sharpen this further. The right answer for "internal tech reference photos" is very different from "evidence for a $50k warranty claim three years from now."
